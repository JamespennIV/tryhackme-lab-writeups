# Lessons Learned

## Overview

This document pulls together the main lessons I took away from the TryHackMe rooms documented in this repository.

The completed rooms:

- Letter
- AI Threat Modeling Assessment
- Missing Person
- Compiled
- Juicy Details

Together, these rooms helped build beginner-level skills across investigation, OSINT, threat modeling, reverse engineering, log analysis, and cybersecurity documentation.

---

## 1. Structured Thinking Matters

Across every room, the most valuable skill wasn't technical — it was not rushing to an answer. Investigation-based labs demanded careful reading, clue identification, evidence comparison, and documented reasoning at every step.

### Lesson

Cybersecurity work depends on structured thinking just as much as it depends on technical tooling.

---

## 2. Evidence Should Drive Conclusions

Rooms like **Letter** and **Missing Person** made it clear how easy it is to follow assumptions instead of facts — and how costly that can be in an investigation.

### Lesson

A strong investigation is built on verified details, not gut feelings.

---

## 3. Logs Tell a Story

**Juicy Details** showed how much information is hiding inside log data — attacker behavior, suspicious patterns, timelines, and web activity all become visible when you know what to look for.

### Lesson

Log analysis is a core cybersecurity skill because logs answer the questions that matter most: what happened, when it happened, and what the evidence says.

---

## 4. Reverse Engineering Requires Patience

**Compiled** introduced beginner binary analysis and made it clear that the obvious clues aren't always the right ones. Visible strings can mislead, and surface-level observations don't always reflect what the program is actually doing.

### Lesson

Reverse engineering is about how a program actually behaves — not just what it looks like at first glance.

---

## 5. AI Security Is an Emerging Area

**AI Threat Modeling Assessment** introduced security risks specific to AI systems — misuse cases, data exposure, prompt-related vulnerabilities, and concerns around system integrations.

### Lesson

AI security means thinking about the whole picture: the model, the data, the users, the prompts, the outputs, and the architecture surrounding all of it.

---

## 6. Documentation Turns Practice Into Portfolio Evidence

Finishing a lab is useful. Documenting it well is what makes it valuable.

### Lesson

Good documentation shows communication skill, technical understanding, and the ability to explain methodology clearly to someone who wasn't in the room with you.

---

## Interview-Relevant Takeaway

These rooms built foundational cybersecurity skills across investigation, analysis, documentation, and technical reasoning — and they give me concrete, specific examples to draw from when talking through hands-on experience in an interview.
